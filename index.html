Cases

The Elgin Marbles

The Elgin Marbles are named after the seventh Lord of Elgin who took them away from Greece. His name was Thomas Bruce. At that time in the early 1800s, the Ottoman Empire was occupying Greece. Thomas Bruce as an ambassador asked the Sultan for permission to take the sculptures. He then got a document which allowed him to take small stones. That document had been translated before it got to the Lord. He finished exporting them in 1805. Latter in 1816 the British Museum purchased the collection reluctantly because of legal reasons. The Lord of Elgin wasn't rich at the time, and the British Museum did not pay much. They then cleaned it but, when doing so, they destroyed tiny details. They did this because at the time they did not know that Greek sculpture was painted. Currently Greece has been trying to get the sculpture back saying that the deal was dubious. The British Museum says that their having the marbles helped enfore the Philhellenic movement. They also say that Greek history isn't just for Greece but Europe, too.

Early 1800s

The Kouroi of Klenia

The Kouroi of Klenia were illegely excavated in a village in Corinth. When the smugglers were found the police arrested them in 2010. The police say that the smugglers demanded €10,000,000 for the Kouroi. The two Kouroi are now located at the Archaeological Museum of Ancient Corinth.

2010-2011

The Sarpedon Krater

The Sarpedon Krater was found in an Etruscan tomb in Cerveteri. It was made near Athens by the Greek painter Euphronis in 515 BCE. It was looted by a man named Giacomo Medici in the early 1970s. Looters normally destroy everything in a tomb just to find something valuable for the art market. The vase was purchased by an art dealer and then purchased by the Metropolitan Museum of Art. They were offered by the dealer a coverstory that it was first owned by a Lebanese art collector then bought by his grandfather before being sold to the museum. The Italian authorities found out but they had no legal rights to the vase. After that the Metropolitan Museum of Art was shamed into returning it. It is now located in the Etruscan Museum in Rome.

early 1970s

Ways to Help

Greek heritage is many things: a culture, a history, art, and information. It is being and has been pillaged, treated illegally, and degraded. I believe that you should take an action. A very simple action would be to tell your friends. Educating people and letting people know is a very effective way to get people to focus on this problem. Another effective action would be donating to an organization called Saving Antiquities for Everyone (SAFE). This organization educates people and works with people to save antiquities. An action that requires more effort is to write to the U.S. Cultural Property Advisory Committee. They will alert the president about the issues involving culture and heritage. The U.S. government takes public opinion very seriously.

In conclusion, it is important and also easy to take an action. It is also important to understand that problems that include the loss of heritage in Greece exist.
